# Rhythm and Flow

Prose rhythm is the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables, sentence lengths, and pauses. It affects pace, mood, and memorability — even in non-fiction.

## The Three Rhythms

### 1. Staccato — Short, Clipped
Short sentences, hard stops, minimal connectives. Creates tension, urgency, or bluntness.

> "He looked at the door. It was closed. He listened. Nothing. He took a step. The floor creaked."

Each period is a beat. The reader's eye snaps from stop to stop.

### 2. Legato — Flowing, Connected
Longer sentences with subordinate clauses and connectives. Creates momentum, reflection, or lyricism.

> "He looked at the door, which was closed as it had been all evening, and he listened — there was nothing, or perhaps there was something just beneath the threshold of hearing — so he took a step forward, and the floor creaked."

The clauses flow one into the next; the reader glides.

### 3. Mixed — Natural
Most good prose alternates. A long, flowing passage builds atmosphere; a short sentence breaks it for impact.

> "He looked at the door, which was closed as it had been all evening, and he listened — nothing, or perhaps there was something just beneath the threshold of hearing — so he took a step forward. The floor creaked. He froze."

The short sentences at the end land like punches.

## The Sound of Sentences

### Alliteration and Assonance

**Alliteration** (repeated consonant sounds) and **assonance** (repeated vowel sounds) create subliminal music:

> "The fair breeze blew, the white foam flew, / The furrow followed free."
— Samuel Taylor Coleridge, *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner*

The F-sounds mimic the rush of wind and water.

> "Do not go gentle into that good night."
— Dylan Thomas, *Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night*

The repeated G and N sounds (go, gentle, good, night) create a sense of inevitability and weight. The line is heavy in the mouth.

### Consonance and Dissonance

Hard consonants (K, T, P, G) create friction. Soft consonants (L, M, N, S, W) create smoothness.

❌ The contract requires strict compliance with complex corporate protocols. *(hard — bureaucratic, aggressive)*
✅ The agreement asks that we follow a few shared principles. *(soft — collaborative, welcoming)*

Neither is universally "better" — the choice should match the message.

## The Power of the Polysyndeton

**Polysyndeton:** Deliberately repeating conjunctions *(and, or, but)* between every item.

> "We have spoken of the sun and the moon and the stars and the earth beneath our feet."
— Paraphrase of biblical cadence

The repeated *and* creates a sense of accumulation, abundance, or exhaustion. Each item feels equally weighty.

**Asyndeton:** Deliberately omitting conjunctions.

> "I came, I saw, I conquered."
— Julius Caesar (via Suetonius)

The omission of *and* creates speed, decisiveness, and finality.

## Famous Examples of Masterful Rhythm

### Dickens — The Long-Breath Opener
> "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair..."

Charles Dickens, *A Tale of Two Cities*

Notice the rhythm: *best/worst, wisdom/foolishness, belief/incredulity, Light/Darkness, hope/despair*. It's anaphora (repeated opening) + antithesis (paired opposites) + crescendo. The passage builds and builds, and the reader is carried by the wave.

### DeLillo — The Hypnotic Fragment
> "The smoke. The crowd. The sirens. The paper drifting down. He walked and kept walking."

Don DeLillo, *Falling Man*

Four sentence fragments. Each is a snapshot. The rhythm mimics the disorientation of the event — the brain processing in disconnected images.

### Didion — The Long Sentence That Breathes
> "I am trying to think if there is anything else I remember about the cerebral cortex, which I studied in a neuroanatomy class at Cal, an impossibly hard class, and I recall that the cerebral cortex is the part of the brain that distinguishes us from the animals, that gives us the capacity to reason and to reflect, and that is why we are able to watch, say, a television documentary about the horrors of war and then get up and make a sandwich."

Joan Didion, *The Year of Magical Thinking*

One sentence, 95 words. It starts in a clinical register (*cerebral cortex, neuroanatomy*) and collapses into the domestic (*make a sandwich*). The rhythm of the sentence — its inexorable slide from the academic to the mundane — is the argument. The form is the meaning.

## Practical Checks for Your Own Prose

1. **Read aloud.** If you stumble, your reader will too.
2. **Count the stressed beats** in your most important sentence. Prose doesn't need strict meter, but stressed syllables should land with intention.
3. **Look for accidental rhymes** — they distract unless deliberate.
4. **Check the ratio.** A paragraph of all long sentences needs one short one. A paragraph of all short sentences needs one that stretches.
5. **End sentences on strong words.** Avoid trailing off with prepositions or weak modifiers — unless the trailing-off is the effect you want.

✅ He knew the truth. It was unbearable. *(final word: "unbearable" — lands hard)*
❌ He knew the truth. It was something he couldn't put up with. *(final word: "with" — drifts away)*
