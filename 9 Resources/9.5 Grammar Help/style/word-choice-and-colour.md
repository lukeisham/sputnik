# Word Choice and Colour

Precision in word choice is the difference between a sketch and a painting. Every word carries denotation (literal meaning) and connotation (emotional weight, cultural association, tone).

## Denotation vs Connotation

| Word | Denotation | Connotation |
|---|---|---|
| slender | thin | positive — graceful, elegant |
| skinny | thin | negative — undernourished, scrawny |
| slim | thin | positive — trim, attractive |
| gaunt | thin | negative — haggard, ill |
| determined | strong-willed | positive — resolute |
| stubborn | strong-willed | negative — inflexible, unreasonable |
| assertive | confident | positive — self-assured |
| aggressive | confident | negative — hostile, pushy |

Choosing the wrong word changes the meaning — not just the mood.

✅ She was **slender** and moved with ease. *(admiring)*
❌ She was **gaunt** and moved with ease. *(contradicts — gaunt implies illness)*

## The Ladder of Abstraction

S.I. Hayakawa's concept: move up for overview, down for detail.

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| **Abstract** | wealth |
| | asset |
| | property |
| | house |
| | bungalow |
| **Concrete** | the peeling yellow bungalow on Ash Street with the broken mailbox |

Good writing moves fluidly between levels:
✅ He was wealthy. *(abstract — sets the scene)* His fortune was built on a single patent. *(mid-level)* The patent was for a brass nozzle that could spray paint in a perfectly even coat — the kind of thing no one notices but everyone depends on. *(concrete)*

## Sensory Language

Abstract words tell. Concrete sensory words show.

| Abstract (Telling) | Concrete (Showing) |
|---|---|
| The room was unpleasant. | The room smelled of damp wool and old cigarettes. |
| She was angry. | Her jaw tightened and her fingers curled around the pen until the plastic creaked. |
| The food was good. | The bread was still warm, its crust crackling under the knife, steam rising from the soft centre. |
| It was a cold day. | Frost spiderwebbed across the windscreen; her breath hung in the air. |

## Famous Examples of Precise Word Choice

### Nabokov — Synesthetic Precision
> "Lolita, light of my life, fire of my loins. My sin, my soul. Lo-lee-ta: the tip of the tongue taking a trip of three steps down the palate to tap, at three, on the teeth. Lo. Lee. Ta."

Vladimir Nabokov, *Lolita*

Nabokov maps sound to physical sensation. The sentence doesn't describe — it performs.

### Orwell — Anti-Precision as Critique
> "The statement 'I have not been negligent in my duties' is rendered 'I have not been remiss in the execution of my responsibilities with regard to...' and then 'I have been a good steward of the resources entrusted to me...' until the truth has been thoroughly obscured."

Paraphrase of George Orwell, *Politics and the English Language*

Orwell's point: bad word choice isn't just ugly — it's dishonest. Vague language protects the speaker from accountability.

### Toni Morrison — Single Words That Carry Worlds
> "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom."

Toni Morrison, *Beloved*

Two sentences. *Spiteful* for a house, *venom* for a baby — both unexpected, both precise. The wrongness of the word choices is the point.

## Weak Words to Interrogate

| Weak Word | Stronger Alternative (context-dependent) |
|---|---|
| nice | pleasant, charming, gracious, thoughtful |
| good | excellent, effective, skilled, satisfying |
| bad | harmful, incompetent, unpleasant, corrosive |
| thing | item, object, concept, issue, element |
| stuff | material, substance, belongings, content |
| very | *(delete; use a stronger adjective)* |
| really | *(delete; use a stronger adverb)* |
| a lot | many, numerous, frequently, substantially |
| got | obtained, received, became, understood |
| interesting | *(say what's interesting about it)* |
| important | vital, crucial, essential, central *(or better: say why it matters)* |

## The "One Perfect Word" Fallacy

Flaubert famously preached *le mot juste* — the one right word. But in practice, there's rarely one perfect word. There are better and worse choices, and the right one depends on rhythm, register, and intention:

✅ He **walked** into the room. *(neutral)*
✅ He **strode** into the room. *(confident)*
✅ He **shuffled** into the room. *(tired, old)*
✅ He **slunk** into the room. *(ashamed, furtive)*
✅ He **burst** into the room. *(urgent, dramatic)*

None are "wrong" — each creates a different character and scene.

## Verdict

Interrogate every adjective and verb in a second draft. If a word is doing real work — carrying weight, setting tone, distinguishing meaning — keep it. If it's filler, cut it. If it's carrying the wrong connotation, replace it.

✅ The investigation **uncovered** evidence of fraud.
❌ The investigation **found** evidence of fraud. *(weaker — "found" is generic)*
✅ The investigation **unearthed** evidence of fraud. *(strong, but slightly sensational)*
✅ The investigation **revealed** evidence of fraud. *(balanced — precise, not melodramatic)*
