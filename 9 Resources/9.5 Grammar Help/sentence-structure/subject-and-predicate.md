# Subject and Predicate

Every complete sentence has two essential parts: a **subject** (who or what the sentence is about) and a **predicate** (what is said about the subject).

## Definitions

- **Simple Subject** — The main noun or pronoun (without modifiers).
- **Complete Subject** — The simple subject plus all its modifiers.
- **Simple Predicate** — The main verb or verb phrase.
- **Complete Predicate** — The verb plus all objects, complements, and modifiers.

| Sentence | Simple Subject | Simple Predicate |
|---|---|---|
| The old brown dog barked loudly. | dog | barked |
| Several of my closest friends have arrived. | Several | have arrived |
| Running every day builds stamina. | Running | builds |

## Compound Subjects and Predicates

- **Compound Subject** — Two or more subjects sharing the same verb.
  ✅ **Tom and Jerry** went to the store.
  ✅ **Neither the rain nor the wind** could stop them.

- **Compound Predicate** — Two or more verbs sharing the same subject.
  ✅ She **wrote the essay and submitted it** on time.
  ✅ He **arrived early, checked in, and waited** patiently.

## Finding the Subject

1. The subject is never in a prepositional phrase.
   - ❌ The box **of chocolates** are on the table. *(subject is "box" — singular)*
   - ✅ The box of chocolates **is** on the table.

2. In questions, the subject follows the auxiliary verb.
   - ✅ **Have** the students finished? *(subject: students)*

3. In inverted sentences, the subject follows the verb.
   - ✅ Here **comes** the bus. *(subject: bus)*

## Subject-Verb Agreement Edge Cases

✅ Each of the books **is** interesting. *(subject: Each)*
✅ The team **is** playing well. *(collective — singular)*
✅ The team **are** arguing among themselves. *(collective acting individually)*
✅ Either the manager or the employees **are** responsible.
✅ Either the employees or the manager **is** responsible. *(nearest subject)*

❌ The bouquet of roses smell wonderful. *(subject: bouquet — singular)*
