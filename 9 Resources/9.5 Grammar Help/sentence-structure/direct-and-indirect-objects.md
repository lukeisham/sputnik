# Direct and Indirect Objects

Objects receive the action of a verb. Understanding them is key to parsing sentence structure.

## Direct Object (DO)

The direct object answers "what?" or "whom?" after the verb. It receives the action directly.

✅ She read **the book**. *(read what? → the book)*
✅ They invited **us**. *(invited whom? → us)*
✅ He built **a house**. *(built what? → a house)*

## Indirect Object (IO)

The indirect object answers "to whom?" or "for whom?" the action is done. It comes **before** the direct object (or after it with *to*/*for*).

✅ She gave **me** the book. *(gave to whom? → me; DO: the book)*
✅ She gave the book **to me**. *(alternate form with preposition)*
✅ He bought **his daughter** a car. *(bought for whom? → his daughter)*
❌ She gave to me the book. *(awkward — "to me" goes at the end)*

## Identifying Objects — Parsing

| Sentence | Subject | Verb | IO | DO |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The teacher handed the student a pencil. | teacher | handed | student | pencil |
| They sent us an invitation. | They | sent | us | invitation |
| She showed her friends the photo. | She | showed | friends | photo |

## Transitive vs Intransitive Verbs

- **Transitive verbs** take a direct object.
  ✅ She threw **the ball**. *(transitive)*
  ❌ She threw. *(incomplete — needs object)*

- **Intransitive verbs** do not take an object.
  ✅ She slept peacefully.
  ✅ The sun rises.

- **Ditransitive verbs** take both an indirect and direct object.
  ✅ He told me a story. *(IO: me, DO: story)*
  ✅ She offered her guest a drink.

## Prepositional Object (vs Indirect Object)

Do not confuse an indirect object with the object of a preposition:

- Indirect object: ✅ She gave **me** the book. *(no preposition)*
- Prepositional object: ✅ She gave the book **to me**. *(me is object of "to")*

Both are correct; the indirect-object-first form is more concise.

✅ He threw the dog the ball.
✅ He threw the ball to the dog.
❌ He threw the dog. *(different meaning!)*
