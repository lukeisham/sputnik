# Fewer vs Less

The traditional rule is simple: *fewer* is for countable nouns; *less* is for uncountable (mass) nouns. But real usage is more nuanced.

## Traditional Rule

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| **Fewer** — things you can count individually | ✅ fewer apples, fewer people, fewer mistakes, fewer cars |
| **Less** — things measured as a quantity or degree | ✅ less water, less time, less money, less patience |

## The Quick Test

Can you put a number directly in front of it?

- ✅ "3 apples" → countable → **fewer apples**
- ✅ "3 traffic" → doesn't work → not countable → **less traffic**

## Modern Nuances

### 1. Distance, Time, Money, Weight — Singular Measurements
Even though *miles, hours, dollars, pounds* are technically countable, they're treated as quantities:

✅ It's **less** than five miles to the station. *(a distance — singular quantity)*
✅ The project took **less** than three hours. *(a duration — singular quantity)*
✅ I paid **less** than ten dollars. *(an amount — singular quantity)*
✅ She weighs **less** than 120 pounds. *(a weight — singular quantity)*

In these cases, *fewer* would sound pedantic ("fewer than five miles").

### 2. The Supermarket Checkout Sign
The sign "10 items or fewer" is traditionally correct (items are countable), but "10 items or less" is so common it's widely accepted. Many style guides now accept both.

### 3. "One Less" vs "One Fewer"
When reducing by exactly one, *less* is idiomatic:

✅ I have one **less** problem now.
?? I have one **fewer** problem now. *(grammatically more precise, but sounds unusual)*

## Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|---|---|
| I have less books than you. | I have **fewer** books than you. |
| There are less people here today. | There are **fewer** people here today. |
| Drink fewer water. | Drink **less** water. |
| I have fewer patience for this. | I have **less** patience for this. |
| She earns less dollars per hour. | She earns **fewer** dollars per hour. *(but: She earns **less** money.)* |

## The Trend

In speech and informal writing, *less* is increasingly used for countables ("less people," "less cars"), especially in British English. In formal writing, follow the traditional rule: *fewer* for countables.

✅ We need **fewer** meetings and **less** bureaucracy.
❌ We need **less** meetings and **fewer** bureaucracy. *(wrong both ways)*

✅ There were **fewer** than 50 attendees.
✅ The event drew **less** than 50 attendees. *(50 treated as a quantity — borderline acceptable)*

## Verdict

In formal or professional writing, use:
- **Fewer** for individual countable items.
- **Less** for unquantifiable mass, abstractions, and singular measurements.

In casual contexts, few people will notice or care.
