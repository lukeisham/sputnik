# That vs Which

These two relative pronouns are frequently confused. The distinction hinges on whether the clause is restrictive (essential) or non-restrictive (extra information).

## The Core Rule

| Pronoun | Clause Type | Commas? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **that** | Restrictive (essential to meaning) | ❌ No commas | The book **that** won the prize is on sale. |
| **which** | Non-restrictive (extra info) | ✅ Commas | The book, **which** won the prize, is on sale. |

## Restrictive Clauses (*That*)

A restrictive clause identifies which specific thing you're talking about. Without it, the meaning changes.

✅ The car **that** is parked in the driveway belongs to my sister. *(identifies which car)*
✅ Please bring me the report **that** I left on your desk. *(identifies which report)*
✅ Students **that** fail the exam must retake it. *(identifies which students)*

Without the clause: *"The car belongs to my sister"* — different meaning.

## Non-restrictive Clauses (*Which*)

A non-restrictive clause adds extra information. The sentence still makes sense without it. Always set off with commas.

✅ The car, **which** is parked in the driveway, belongs to my sister. *(there's only one car; the location is extra info)*
✅ The report, **which** I left on your desk, needs your signature. *(extra context)*
✅ The Amazon, **which** is the world's largest river by volume, flows through South America. *(there's only one Amazon)*

## Quick Test

1. Remove the clause. Does the sentence still identify the same thing?
   - Yes → it's non-restrictive → use **which** with commas.
   - No → it's restrictive → use **that** without commas.

2. Can you delete the clause without changing what you're pointing at?
   - "The car **that** is red is mine." *(If there are multiple cars, removing "that is red" changes which car → restrictive → **that**)*
   - "My car, **which** is red, is parked outside." *("My car" already identifies it → non-restrictive → **which**)*

## Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|---|---|
| The policy which was introduced last year is flawed. | The policy **that** was introduced last year is flawed. *(restrictive — identifies which policy)* |
| My house that I bought in 2010 needs renovating. | My house, **which** I bought in 2010, needs renovating. *(I only have one house — non-restrictive)* |
| The team which won the championship celebrated. | The team **that** won the championship celebrated. *(restrictive — identifies which team)* |

## UK vs US Usage

- **US English:** Strictly distinguishes *that* (restrictive) and *which* (non-restrictive).
- **UK English:** Often uses *which* for both, relying on commas to signal the distinction.
  - ✅ The book which won the prize is on sale. *(UK — acceptable without commas if restrictive)*
  - ✅ The book, which won the prize, is on sale. *(non-restrictive)*

In international or US-facing professional writing, follow the US convention.

## *That* for People?

Traditionally, *who* is used for people, *that* for things. In practice, *that* for people is acceptable in restrictive clauses:

✅ The woman **who** lives next door is a doctor. *(traditional)*
✅ The woman **that** lives next door is a doctor. *(acceptable)*
✅ The woman, **who** lives next door, is a doctor. *(non-restrictive — "that" would be wrong here)*

❌ The woman, **that** lives next door, is a doctor. *(never use "that" in a non-restrictive clause)*

## Verdict

Use *that* when the clause is essential to identifying what you're talking about. Use *which* (with commas) when the clause is bonus information. If you're unsure, try the deletion test.

✅ The version **that** we shipped on Monday has a bug.
✅ Version 2.0, **which** we shipped on Monday, has a bug. *(only one Version 2.0)*
❌ The version **which** we shipped on Monday has a bug. *(ambiguous — restrictive should use "that")*
