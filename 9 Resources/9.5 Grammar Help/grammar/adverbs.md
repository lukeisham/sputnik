# Adverbs

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. They answer the questions *How?*, *When?*, *Where?*, *How often?*, and *To what extent?* Many adverbs end in *-ly*, but not all do.

## Types of Adverbs

| Type | Questions Answered | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Manner** | How? | *quickly, softly, carefully, well* |
| **Time** | When? | *now, yesterday, soon, later* |
| **Place** | Where? | *here, there, inside, everywhere* |
| **Degree** | To what extent? | *very, almost, quite, extremely* |
| **Frequency** | How often? | *always, never, sometimes, daily* |

## Key Rules

1. **Placement Matters** — The position of an adverb can change the emphasis or meaning of a sentence.
2. **Adverbs of Manner** — Usually go after the verb or after the object (if there is one).
3. **Adverbs of Frequency** — Usually go before the main verb but after forms of *to be*.
4. **Adverbs of Degree** — Usually go directly before the word they modify.

### Mid-Position (Frequency & Degree)
✅ She **always** arrives on time.
✅ He **never** eats meat.
✅ They **are** **always** happy to help.

### End-Position (Manner & Place)
✅ She sang **beautifully**.
✅ He put the book **there**.
✅ They walked **slowly** through the park.

### Front-Position (Time & Emphasis)
✅ **Yesterday**, we went to the beach.
✅ **Suddenly**, the lights went out.

## Comparative and Superlative Adverbs

| Positive | Comparative | Superlative |
|---|---|---|
| fast | faster | fastest |
| carefully | more carefully | most carefully |
| well | better | best |
| badly | worse | worst |
| early | earlier | earliest |

## Common Mistakes

### Good vs Well
**Good** is an adjective; **well** is an adverb (except when describing health).

✅ She did a **good** job. (adjective modifying *job*)
✅ She did **well** on the exam. (adverb modifying *did*)
✅ I don't **feel well**. (health — adjective)
❌ She did **good** on the exam. (adverb needed)

### Misplaced Adverbs
Place the adverb as close as possible to the word it modifies to avoid ambiguity.

✅ **Only** I said I loved her. (no one else said it)
✅ I **only** said I loved her. (I said nothing else)
✅ I said **only** I loved her. (I love no one else)

### Splitting Infinitives (Stylistic)
✅ She decided **to quickly finish** the report. (acceptable in modern usage)
⚠️ She decided **to finish quickly** the report. (awkward — avoid)

### Redundant Adverbs
✅ They **returned** to the hotel.
❌ They **returned back** to the hotel.
✅ She **repeated** the question.
❌ She **repeated again** the question.
