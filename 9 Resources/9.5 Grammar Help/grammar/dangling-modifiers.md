# Dangling and Misplaced Modifiers

A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that describes another element in the sentence. When a modifier is placed too far from what it describes — or when what it describes is missing entirely — the sentence becomes confusing or unintentionally humorous.

## Dangling Modifiers

A dangling modifier is a descriptive phrase that has **nothing to modify** in the sentence. The implied subject is missing.

✅ **Walking down the street**, I saw a beautiful garden. *(I was walking)*
❌ **Walking down the street**, the garden was beautiful. *(gardens don't walk)*

✅ **After reading the report**, she understood the problem.
❌ **After reading the report**, the problem was clear. *(problems don't read)*

✅ **To succeed in business**, you need determination.
❌ **To succeed in business**, determination is needed. *(determination doesn't succeed)*

### Fixing Dangling Modifiers

| Strategy | Example |
|---|---|
| Add the implied subject | ❌ **Having finished the assignment**, the TV was turned on. → ✅ **Having finished the assignment**, I turned on the TV. |
| Change the dangling phrase to a clause | ❌ **While driving to work**, a deer crossed the road. → ✅ **While I was driving to work**, a deer crossed the road. |
| Combine the modifier with the subject | ❌ **Exhausted after the workout**, the couch looked inviting. → ✅ **Exhausted after the workout**, I found the couch inviting. |

## Misplaced Modifiers

A misplaced modifier is correctly describing something, but it's in the wrong position, creating ambiguity.

### Limiting Modifiers (*only, just, nearly, almost, merely*)

Place these immediately before the word they modify:

✅ **Only** she lent me five dollars. *(no one else lent)*
✅ She **only** lent me five dollars. *(she didn't give it)*
✅ She lent **only** me five dollars. *(no one else got a loan)*
✅ She lent me **only** five dollars. *(not more than five)*

❌ She only lent me five dollars. *(ambiguous — did she only lend, or lend only to me, or lend only five?)*

### Ambiguous Modification

❌ The waiter served a steak to the customer that was undercooked.
✅ The waiter served an undercooked steak to the customer.
✅ The waiter served a steak that was undercooked to the customer.

❌ She almost drove her kids to school every day.
✅ She drove her kids to school almost every day. *(frequency, not an attempt to drive)*

❌ I saw a bear hiking in the mountains.
✅ While hiking in the mountains, I saw a bear. *(the bear wasn't hiking — probably)*

## Squinting Modifiers

A squinting modifier sits between two elements and could modify either:

❌ Students who study **often** get better grades.
- Does "often" modify "study" (students who frequently study)?
- Or does "often" modify "get" (students who study frequently get better grades)?

✅ Students who study frequently tend to get better grades.
✅ Students who study tend to get better grades often.

## Participial Phrase Position

✅ **Smiling broadly**, she accepted the award.
✅ She accepted the award, **smiling broadly**.
❌ She accepted, **smiling broadly**, the award. *(awkward split)*

✅ The professor, **known for her rigorous standards**, assigned another paper.
❌ The professor assigned another paper, **known for her rigorous standards**. *(modifier too far from "professor")*

## Quick Test

Ask: "Who or what is doing the action in the modifier?" If the answer isn't the subject of the main clause, the modifier is dangling.

❌ **To apply for the job**, a résumé must be submitted.
- Who applies? A résumé doesn't apply.
✅ **To apply for the job**, you must submit a résumé.
