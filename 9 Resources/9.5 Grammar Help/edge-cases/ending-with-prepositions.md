# Ending Sentences with Prepositions

The "rule" against ending sentences with prepositions is one of the most persistent grammar myths. English is a Germanic language — preposition stranding is a natural feature.

## The Origin

17th-century poet John Dryden criticized Ben Jonson for ending a sentence with a preposition. 18th-century grammarians codified it as a "rule" to make English more like Latin — where prepositions literally cannot end sentences. The rule has no basis in English grammar.

## When Ending with a Preposition Is Natural

### 1. Phrasal Verbs
✅ Please **turn** the lights **off**. / Please **turn off** the lights. *(both fine)*
✅ What are you **looking for**?
❌ For what are you looking? *(stilted — unless being deliberately formal)*

### 2. Passive Constructions
✅ She was **laughed at**.
❌ At she was laughed. *(nonsensical)*

### 3. Questions
✅ Who are you **talking to**?
❌ To whom are you talking? *(grammatical but very formal)*

### 4. Relative Clauses
✅ This is the book I was **looking for**.
❌ This is the book for which I was looking. *(pedantic in most contexts)*

### 5. Infinitive Phrases
✅ She needs someone to **talk to**.
❌ She needs someone to whom to talk. *(absurdly formal)*

### 6. Idiomatic Expressions
✅ Let's **call it off**.
✅ They **got away with** it.
✅ I can't **put up with** this.

## When to Avoid (Formal Writing)

In extremely formal writing (legal, academic, ceremonial), you may want to avoid stranded prepositions — not because they're wrong, but because some readers still expect it:

| Informal (Correct) | Formal Alternative |
|---|---|
| the team I work with | the team with which I work |
| the outcome we hoped for | the outcome for which we hoped |
| a problem we hadn't thought of | a problem of which we hadn't thought |

## The Churchill Anecdote (Probably Apocryphal)

> "This is the sort of English up with which I will not put."

The story goes that an editor "corrected" Churchill's stranded preposition and he responded with this deliberately awkward sentence. Whether true or not, it illustrates the absurdity of rigidly obeying this fake rule.

## Verdict

Ending a sentence with a preposition is **grammatically correct in English**. In formal writing, you can choose to avoid it — but only if the result still sounds natural. Forcing a preposition before its object often creates stilted, unnatural prose that is worse than the "problem" it solves.

✅ That's the team I work **with**.
✅ That's the team with which I work. *(acceptable in formal contexts)*
❌ That's the team with I work. *(wrong — need "which")*

✅ What are you waiting **for**?
❌ For what are you waiting? *(unnatural in speech)*
