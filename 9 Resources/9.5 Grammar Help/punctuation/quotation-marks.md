# Quotation Marks

Quotation marks enclose direct speech, titles of short works, and words used in a special sense. American and British conventions differ.

## Direct Quotations

✅ She said, "The meeting is at noon."
✅ "The meeting," she said, "is at noon."
❌ She said "The meeting is at noon." *(missing comma before quote)*

## Punctuation Inside vs Outside (US Convention)

| Mark | Inside Quotes? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Comma / Period | ✅ Always inside | "Hello," she said. / She said, "Hello." |
| Semicolon / Colon | ❌ Always outside | She read "The Raven"; then she left. |
| Question / Exclamation | Depends on meaning | She asked, "Are you coming?" *(quote is the question)* / Did she say, "I'm coming"? *(sentence is the question)* |

## Single vs Double Quotes

- **US:** Double quotes for primary, single for quotes within quotes.
  ✅ "She told me, 'Wait here,' and left."
- **UK:** Often reverses — single outside, double inside.
  ✅ 'She told me, "Wait here," and left.'

## Titles of Short Works (Quotation Marks)

Use quotation marks for:
- Articles: "The Case for Boredom"
- Poems: "The Road Not Taken"
- Short stories: "The Lottery"
- Songs: "Bohemian Rhapsody"
- TV episodes: "The One with the Prom Video"

Use *italics* for books, albums, films, TV series, and periodicals.

## Scare Quotes and Special Usage

Use quotation marks to indicate irony, doubt, or an unfamiliar term (but overuse looks amateurish):

✅ The so-called "expert" had no credentials.
✅ The "cloud" refers to remote servers.
❌ We "invite" you to our "amazing" "event." *(overuse)*

## Dialogue Formatting

```
"Did you finish the report?" she asked.
"Yes," he replied, "I sent it this morning."
"Good. The client has been waiting."
```

- New speaker = new paragraph.
- Action beats use periods (not comma attribution).

✅ "I'm not sure." He scratched his chin. "Let me think."
❌ "I'm not sure," he scratched his chin, "let me think." *(action beat, not dialogue tag)*
