# Colons

A colon introduces what follows: a list, an explanation, a quotation, or an elaboration.

## Key Uses

### 1. Introducing a List
✅ Bring three items: a tent, a flashlight, and water.
❌ Bring: a tent, a flashlight, and water. *(colon after verb — usually wrong)*

**Rule:** Only use a colon after a **complete sentence** that introduces a list.

### 2. Introducing an Explanation or Elaboration
✅ She had one goal: to win.
✅ The reason is simple: we ran out of time.

### 3. Between Independent Clauses (Second Explains First)
✅ The decision was final: there would be no appeal.
✅ He learned a hard lesson: trust must be earned.

### 4. Introducing a Quotation
✅ The speaker began with a challenge: "Are you ready?"
✅ Lincoln's Gettysburg Address opens memorably: "Four score and seven years ago..."

### 5. Titles and Subtitles
✅ *Sputnik: A Grammar Guide*
✅ *Star Wars: Episode IV — A New Hope*

### 6. Time, Ratios, and Citations
✅ 10:30 a.m.
✅ The vote was 3:1.
✅ John 3:16

## Capitalization After a Colon

| Style | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **US** | Capitalize if what follows is a complete sentence | ✅ He made a discovery: The data was flawed. |
| **UK** | Lowercase regardless | ✅ He made a discovery: the data was flawed. |

Both are acceptable — be consistent.

## Common Errors

✅ She wanted three things: success, fame, and wealth. *(complete clause before colon)*
❌ She wanted: success, fame, and wealth. *(colon interrupts verb and object)*

✅ The ingredients are: flour, sugar, and eggs. *(after "are" — wrong)*
✅ The ingredients are flour, sugar, and eggs.

✅ There's one rule: always be honest. *(explains the rule)*
❌ There's a problem with: the deadline, the budget, and the team. *(after preposition — wrong)*
